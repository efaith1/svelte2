Dataset

This dataset contains the residential sales data in the city of Boston from the year 2000 to 2023. It includes information about the seller, buyer, price, location, whether the sale constitutes a 'flip', style of the building (e.g high-rise), and more information about residential buildings sold in the city. This dataset is provided by The Warren Group. The analysis will focus on the high end of the price points and on specific hi-rises and luxury housing and explore how an increased development of such properties affect the average Bostonians.

Discoveries and Insights

To assess the data quality and resolution, we first explore the styles of buildings covered by this dataset, and the temporal coverage that the dataset provides. In the context of this analysis, we can start by plotting how many hi-rises were built per year.

We can immmediately see that despite the dataset's promise to show the number of units sold per year, the "year" attribute only shows how many of a given style of building were built in a given year. The time when a property was sold is actually given by the "date" attribute, which is a very confusing phenomenon. The dates also span to 2022 unlike the promised 2023. Nonetheless, we can explore the question of how luxury buildings contribute to a widening housing gap and wealth inequality by asking the following question: how do different investor types contribute to the construction of luxury houses?

To produce this visualization, I had to filter out the styles of buildings to include only high-end properties; namely high-rise, mid-rise, low-rise, condos, and row buildings. Among the different types of investors, non-investors acquire the majority of the square feet taken up by most luxury housing. This knowledge is both intuitive and counterintuitive. It makes sense because I included low rises and condos which are relatively more affordable and there are significantly more average Bostonians than there are the mega-rich, but it is counterintuitive because the readings suggested that high-end luxury apartments had a huge negative impact, which is not reflected here. This prompts me to think that the cost of luxury housing must be astronomically high, which leads me to ask two questions, what is the average price of the types of properties investors buy, and how much is the average sale price of these luxury apartments?

Using the same representation as before lets us starkly see the relationship between the lotsizes of apartments and their prices. Though the majority of buildings by square footage in Boston are bought by non-investors, the price of these buildings is significantly low compared to the price of the few buildings owned by investors. Small, medium, large, and institutional investors contribute the highest average price range of buildings bought in Boston. This explains why they cannot afford to buy a significant amount of buildings given their unsightly price tags.

As a response to the second follow up question posed above, we now investigate the average last sale price of buildings by style. For this visualization, I had to filter off a few building styles whose values were essentially negligible compared to the others. Something interesting to note is that the average sale price of one unit in a high rise is close to that of an entire townhouse or condo. This is still counter-intuitive because I am yet to see the harm of luxury housing since it seems to be a space-efficient building choice. But not everyone can afford or wants to live in a luxury housing everyday, and you would assume the existance of luxury housing has no effect on them, right?

According to these two visualization, the evil of luxury housing lies in its effect on the price of other types of buildings. The more luxury houses are built, the higher the cost of other building types go as well. The first visualization made by filtering the years to only show buildings sold between 2010 and 2021 shows that luxury building sales have been on the rise and continue to go up. Althought the relationships is not definitely causal, we can still see by the second visualization that the price of all building types has been on the rise over the years. But how can we be certain that this relationship is causal?

To answer this follow up question, we shall need to both look into our second main analysis question about the motivation of developers disproportionately focusing on luxury real estate projects in Boston rather than affordable housing options, and draw on the conclusions made in the article “Reckoning with Boston’s Towers of Wealth" by the Boston Globe about the direct correlation between investor involvement in luxury property purchases and dwindling affordable housing numbers. The visualization above was filtered to only include luxury housing like high rises and row houses, and it confirms that investors do in fact take the majority of the "pie" in property sales relating to luxury housing.

From the data shown in the first two visualizations highlighting the inverse relation between square footage and sales price, this visualization supports the logical conclusion that developers would arrive to to build more high rise properties and luxury buildings in general for maximum profit, thus explaining why developers would favor focusing on luxury real estate projects. To answer our first analysis question by tapping into the conclusions of the article “Reckoning with Boston’s Towers of Wealth" by the Boston Globe, the increased production of such buildings at the expense of more affordable housing drives the wedge deeper in Boston's housing gap and wealth inequality.

Our last analysis question looked into the impact that policymakers can have. Luckily, the data shows that this is possible. We have already concluded that the more luxurious houses cost the most, and so this visualization showing the total price of residential buildings by street can help pinpoint the hotspots that developers and investors target, and get some zoning sort of policy applied to them by policymakers. Important to note is that the streets portrayed in this visualization are only those whose net worth is above 50 million dollars, which is a high bar that many streets do not cut.

The analysis underscores the intricate relationship between luxury housing, investor involvement, and its impact on the housing landscape in Boston. While luxury housing may offer space efficiency, its high cost contributes to rising housing prices across the city. Policymakers have the opportunity to use data-driven insights to strike a balance between promoting economic growth through luxury real estate and addressing the pressing need for affordable housing.
